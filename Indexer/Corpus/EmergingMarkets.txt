<p>An <b>emerging market</b> is a country that has some characteristics of a <a href="/wiki/Developed_market" title="Developed market">developed market</a>, but does not meet standards to be a developed market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This includes countries that may become developed markets in the future or were in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frontier_market" title="Frontier market">frontier market</a>" is used for developing countries with slower economies than "emerging".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The economies of <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_China" title="Economy of China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_India" title="Economy of India">India</a> are considered to be the largest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i>, many people find the term outdated, but no new term has gained traction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Emerging market hedge fund capital reached a record new level in the first quarter of 2011 of $121 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The four largest emerging and developing economies by either <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(nominal)" title="List of countries by GDP (nominal)">nominal</a> or <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(PPP)" title="List of countries by GDP (PPP)">PPP-adjusted GDP</a> are the <a href="/wiki/BRIC" title="BRIC">BRIC</a> countries (<a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>). The next five largest markets are <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> (though, considered a developed market), <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Iran" title="Economy of Iran">Iran</a> is also considered an emerging market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_markets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Developing_countries.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="628" data-file-width="1357" height="102" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Developing_countries.png/220px-Developing_countries.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Developing_countries.png/330px-Developing_countries.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Developing_countries.png/440px-Developing_countries.png 2x" width="220" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Developing_countries.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<div class="legend"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #007DCC; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span>  Developing countries that are neither part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_developed_countries" title="Least developed countries">least developed countries</a>, nor of the <a href="/wiki/Newly_industrialized_country" title="Newly industrialized country">newly industrialized countries</a></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the 1970s, "less developed countries" (LDCs) was the common term for markets that were less "developed" (by objective or subjective measures) than the developed countries such as the United States, Western Europe, and Japan. These markets were supposed to provide greater potential for profit, but also more risk from various factors. This term was thought by some to be politically incorrect so the <i>emerging market</i> label was created. The term is misleading in that there is no guarantee that a country will move from "less developed" to "more developed"; although that is the general trend in the world, countries can also move from "more developed" to "less developed".</p>
<p>Originally brought into fashion in the 1980s by then <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> economist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_Van_Agtmael&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Antoine Van Agtmael (page does not exist)">Antoine Van Agtmael</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> the term is sometimes loosely used as a replacement for <i>emerging economies</i>, but really signifies a business phenomenon that is not fully described by or constrained to geography or economic strength; such <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countries" title="Countries">countries</a> are considered to be in a transitional phase between <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed</a> status. Examples of emerging markets include many countries in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, most countries in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, some countries of <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, some countries in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and some countries in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. Emphasizing the fluid nature of the category, political scientist <a href="/wiki/Ian_Bremmer" title="Ian Bremmer">Ian Bremmer</a> defines an emerging market as "a country where politics matters at least as much as economics to the markets".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The research on emerging markets is diffused within <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a> literature. While researchers including, <a href="/wiki/George_Haley" title="George Haley">George Haley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Kvint" title="Vladimir Kvint">Vladimir Kvint</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Soto_(economist)" title="Hernando de Soto (economist)">Hernando de Soto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Usha_Haley" title="Usha Haley">Usha Haley</a>, and several professors from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yale_School_of_Management" title="Yale School of Management">Yale School of Management</a> have described activity in countries such as India and China, how a market emerges is little understood.</p>
<p>In 1999, Dr. Kvint published this definition: "Emerging market country is a society transitioning from a dictatorship to a free-market-oriented-economy, with increasing economic freedom, gradual integration with the Global Marketplace and with other members of the GEM (Global Emerging Market), an expanding middle class, improving standards of living, social stability and tolerance, as well as an increase in cooperation with multilateral institutions"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2008 Emerging Economy Report,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Knowledge_Societies" title="Center for Knowledge Societies">Center for Knowledge Societies</a> defines Emerging Economies as those "regions of the world that are experiencing rapid informationalization under conditions of limited or partial industrialization." It appears that emerging markets lie at the intersection of non-traditional user behavior, the rise of new user groups and community adoption of products and services, and innovations in product technologies and platforms.</p>
<p>More critical scholars have also studied key emerging markets like Mexico and Turkey. Thomas Marois (2012, 2) argues that financial imperatives have become much more significant and has developed the idea of 'emerging finance capitalism' - an era wherein the collective interests of financial capital principally shape the logical options and choices of government and state elites over and above those of labor and popular classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Julien Vercueil recently proposed an pragmatic definition of the "emerging economies", as distinguished from "emerging markets" coined by an approach heavily influenced by financial criteria. According to his definition, an emerging economy displays the following characteristics:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vercueil_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vercueil-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Intermediate income: its PPP per capita income is comprised between 10% and 75% of the average EU per capita income.</li>
</ol>
<ol>
<li>Catching-up growth: during at least the last decade, it has experienced a brisk economic growth that has narrowed the income gap with advanced economies.</li>
</ol>
<ol>
<li>Institutional transformations and economic opening: during the same period, it has undertaken profound institutional transformations which contributed to integrate it more deeply into the world economy. Hence, emerging economies appears to be a by-product of the current globalization.</li>
</ol>
<p>At the beginning of the 2010s, more than 50 countries, representing 60% of the world's population and 45% of its GDP, matched these criteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vercueil_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vercueil-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:10</sup> Among them, the BRICs.</p>

<p>The term "rapidly developing economies" is being used to denote emerging markets such as The <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> that are undergoing rapid growth.</p>
<p>In recent years, new terms have emerged to describe the largest developing countries such as <a href="/wiki/BRIC" title="BRIC">BRIC</a> that stands for <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> along with <i>BRICET</i> (BRIC + Eastern Europe and Turkey), <i>BRICS</i> (BRIC + South Africa), <i>BRICM</i> (BRIC + Mexico), <a href="/wiki/MINT_(economics)" title="MINT (economics)">MINT</a> (Mexico, Indonesia, Nigeria and Turkey), <a href="/wiki/Next_Eleven" title="Next Eleven">Next Eleven</a> (Bangladesh, Egypt, Indonesia, Iran, Mexico, Nigeria, Pakistan, the Philippines, South Korea, Turkey, and Vietnam) and <a href="/wiki/CIVETS" title="CIVETS">CIVETS</a> (Colombia, Indonesia, Vietnam, Egypt, Turkey and South Africa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> These countries do not share any common agenda, but some experts believe that they are enjoying an increasing role in the world economy and on political platforms.</p>
<p>It is difficult to make an exact list of emerging (or developed) markets; the best guides tend to be investment information sources like <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=EMIS_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="EMIS (company) (page does not exist)">EMIS</a></i>, (a Euromoney Institutional Investor Company) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> or market index makers (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morgan_Stanley_Capital_International" title="Morgan Stanley Capital International">Morgan Stanley Capital International</a>). These sources are well-informed, but the nature of investment information sources leads to two potential problems. One is an element of historicity; markets may be maintained in an index for continuity, even if the countries have since developed past the emerging market phase. Possible examples of this are <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kor_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kor-17">[17]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. A second is the simplification inherent in making an index; small countries, or countries with limited market liquidity are often not considered, with their larger neighbours considered an appropriate stand-in.</p>
<p>In an <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Opalesque.TV&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Opalesque.TV (page does not exist)">Opalesque.TV</a> video, <a href="/wiki/Hedge_fund" title="Hedge fund">hedge fund</a> manager Jonathan Binder discusses the current and future relevance of the term "emerging markets" in the financial world. Binder says that in the future investors will not necessarily think of the traditional classifications of "G10" (or G7) versus "emerging markets". Instead, people should look at the world as countries that are fiscally responsible and countries that are not. Whether that country is in Europe or in South America should make no difference, making the traditional "blocs" of categorization irrelevant. Guégan <i>et al.</i> (2014) also discuss the relevance of the terminology "emerging country" comparing the credit worthiness of so-called emerging countries to so-called developed countries. According to their analysis, depending on the criteria used, the term may not always be appropriate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 10 <i>Big Emerging Markets</i> (BEM) economies are (alphabetically ordered): <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> are <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(PPP)" title="List of countries by GDP (PPP)">other major emerging markets</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Newly_industrialized_country" title="Newly industrialized country">Newly industrialized countries</a> are emerging markets whose economies have not yet reached developed status but have, in a macroeconomic sense, outpaced their developing counterparts.</p>
<p>Individual investors can invest in emerging markets by buying into emerging markets or global funds. If they want to pick single stocks or make their own bets they can do it either through ADRs (American depositor Receipts - stocks of foreign companies that trade on US stock exchanges) or through exchange traded funds (exchange traded funds or ETFs hold basket of stocks). The exchange traded funds can be focused on a particular country (e.g., China, India) or region (e.g., Asia-Pacific, Latin America).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="BBVA_Research">BBVA Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_markets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: BBVA Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In November 2010, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBVA" title="BBVA">BBVA</a> Research introduced a new economic concept, to identify a key emerging markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> This classification is divided in two set of developing economies.</p>
<p>As of March 2014, the groupings are as follows:</p>
<p><b>EAGLEs</b> (<a href="/wiki/Emerging_and_growth-leading_economies" title="Emerging and growth-leading economies">emerging and growth-leading economies</a>): Expected Incremental GDP in the next 10 years to be larger than the average of the G7 economies, excluding the US.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emerging_Market_Bond_Index_Global">Emerging Market Bond Index Global</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_markets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Emerging Market Bond Index Global">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Emerging Market Bond Index Global (EMBI Global) by J.P. Morgan was the first comprehensive EM sovereign index in the market, after the EMBI+. It provides full coverage of the EM asset class with representative countries, investable instruments (sovereign and quasi-sovereign), and transparent rules. The EMBI Global includes only USD-denominated emerging markets sovereign bonds and uses a traditional, market capitalization weighted method for country allocation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-30">[30]</a></sup> As of March end 2016, the EMBI Global's market capitalization was $692.3bn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>For country inclusion, a country’s GNI per capita must be below the Index Income Ceiling (IIC) for three consecutive years to be eligible for inclusion to the EMBI Global. J.P. Morgan defines the Index Income Ceiling (IIC) as the GNI per capita level that is adjusted every year by the growth rate of the World GNI per capita, Atlas method (current US$), provided by the World Bank annually. An existing country may be considered for removal from the index if its GNI per capita is above the Index Income Ceiling (IIC) for three consecutive years as well as the country’s long term foreign currency sovereign credit rating (the available ratings from all three agencies: S&P, Moody’s & Fitch) is A-/A3/A- (inclusive) or above for three consecutive years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>J.P. Morgan has introduced what is called an “Index Income Ceiling” (IIC), defined as the income level that is adjusted every year by the growth rate of the World GNI per capita, provided by the World Bank as “GNI per capita, Atlas method (current US$) annually.” Once a country has GNI per capita below or above the IIC level for three consecutive years, the country eligibility will be determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>J.P. Morgan have established the base IIC level in 1987 to match the World Bank High Income threshold at US$6,000 GNI per capita.</li>
<li>Every year, growth in the World GNI per capita figure is applied to the IIC, establishing a new IIC that is dynamic over time.</li>
<li>This approach ensures that our cutoff for index removal is adjusted by the World income growth rate, and not by the inflation rate of a smaller sample of Developed economies.</li>
<li>This metric essentially incorporates real global growth, global inflation, and currency exchange rate (current USD-denominated) changes.</li>
<li>Essentially, the introduction of the IIC establishes a higher, more appropriate threshold for country eligibility in the EMBI Global/Diversified.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Growth_Generators">Global Growth Generators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_markets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Global Growth Generators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Global Growth Generators", or <a href="/wiki/3G_(countries)" title="3G (countries)">3G (countries)</a>, is an alternative classification determined by <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a> analysts as being countries with the most promising growth prospects for 2010-2050. These consist of Indonesia, Egypt, seven other emerging countries, and two countries not previously listed before, specifically <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. There has been disagreement about the reclassification of these countries, among others, for the purpose of acronym creation as was seen with the BRICS.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimating_Demand_in_Emerging_Markets">Estimating Demand in Emerging Markets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_markets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Estimating Demand in Emerging Markets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Estimating the demand for products or services in emerging markets and developing economies can be complex and challenging for managers. These countries have unique commercial environments and may be limited in terms of reliable data, market research firms, and trained interviewers. Consumers in some of these countries may consider surveys an invasion of privacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Survey respondents may try to please researchers by telling them what they want to hear rather than providing honest answers to their questions. However some companies have dedicated their entire business units for understanding the dynamics of emerging markets owing to their peculiarity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
