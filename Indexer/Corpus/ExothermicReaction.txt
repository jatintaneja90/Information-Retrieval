<p>An <b>exothermic reaction</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> that releases <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> by <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> or <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>. It is the opposite of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic_reaction" title="Endothermic reaction">endothermic reaction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Expressed in a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equation" title="Chemical equation">chemical equation</a>: reactants → products + energy</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exothermic_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An exothermic reaction is a chemical or physical reaction that releases heat. It gives net energy to its surroundings. That is, the energy needed to initiate the reaction is less than the energy released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the medium in which the reaction is taking place gains heat, the reaction is exothermic. When using a <a href="/wiki/Calorimeter" title="Calorimeter">calorimeter</a>, the total amount of heat that flows into (or through) the calorimeter is the negative of the net change in energy of the system.</p>
<p>The absolute amount of energy in a chemical system is difficult to measure or calculate. The <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a> change, ΔH, of a chemical reaction is much easier to work with. The enthalpy change equals the change in internal energy of the system plus the work needed to change the volume of the system against constant ambient pressure. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bomb_calorimeter" title="Bomb calorimeter">bomb calorimeter</a> is very suitable for measuring the energy change, ΔH, of a <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> reaction. Measured and calculated ΔH values are related to bond energies by:</p>
<dl>
<dd>ΔH = energy used in forming product bonds − energy released in breaking reactant bonds</dd>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ac_com.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="334" data-file-width="487" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Ac_com.svg/300px-Ac_com.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Ac_com.svg/450px-Ac_com.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Ac_com.svg/600px-Ac_com.svg.png 2x" width="300" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ac_com.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_profile" title="Energy profile">energy profile</a> of an exothermic reaction</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In an exothermic reaction, by definition, the enthalpy change has a negative value:</p>
<dl>
<dd>ΔH < 0</dd>
</dl>
<p>since a larger value (the energy released in the reaction) is subtracted from a smaller value (the energy used for the reaction). For example, when hydrogen burns:</p>
<dl>
<dd>2H<sub>2</sub> (g) + O<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2H<sub>2</sub>O (g)</dd>
<dd>ΔH = −483.6 kJ/mol of O<sub>2</sub> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The most commonly available hand warmers make use of the oxidation of iron to achieve an exothermic reaction:</p>
<dl>
<dd>4Fe(s) + 3O<sub>2</sub>(g) → 2Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>(s).</dd>
</dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_points_to_think_about">Other points to think about</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exothermic_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other points to think about">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The concept and its opposite number <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a> relate to the enthalpy change in any process, not just chemical reactions.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Endergonic_reaction" title="Endergonic reaction">endergonic reactions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exergonic_reaction" title="Exergonic reaction">exergonic reactions</a> it is the sign of the <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs free energy</a> that determines the equilibrium point, and not <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a>. The related concepts <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endergonic" title="Endergonic">endergonic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exergonic" title="Exergonic">exergonic</a> apply to all physical processes.</li>
<li>The conceptually related <a href="/wiki/Warm-blooded" title="Warm-blooded">endotherm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ectotherm" title="Ectotherm">ectotherm</a> (or sometimes exotherm) are concepts in animal physiology.</li>
<li>In quantum numbers, when any excited energy level goes down to its original level for example: when n=4 fall to n=2, energy is released so, it is exothermic.</li>
<li>Where an exothermic reaction causes heating of the reaction vessel which is not controlled, the rate of reaction can increase, in turn causing heat to be evolved even more quickly. This positive feedback situation is known as <a href="/wiki/Thermal_runaway" title="Thermal runaway">thermal runaway</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosion</a> can also result from the problem.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exothermic_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heat production or absorption in either a physical process or chemical reaction is measured using <a href="/wiki/Calorimetry" title="Calorimetry">calorimetry</a>. One common laboratory instrument is the <a href="/wiki/Reaction_calorimeter" title="Reaction calorimeter">reaction calorimeter</a>, where the heat flow into or from the reaction vessel is monitored. The technique can be used to follow chemical reactions as well as physical processes such as <a href="/wiki/Crystallization" title="Crystallization">crystallization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolution</a>.</p>
<p>Energy released is measured in <a href="/wiki/Joule_per_mole" title="Joule per mole">Joule per mole</a>. The reaction has a negative ΔH(<a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> change) value due to heat loss. e.g.: -123 J/mol</p>
