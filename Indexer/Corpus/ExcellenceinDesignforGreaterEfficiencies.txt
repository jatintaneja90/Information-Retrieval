<p>The <b>International Finance Corporation</b> (<b>IFC</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/International_financial_institutions" title="International financial institutions">international financial institution</a> that offers <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">investment</a>, advisory, and <a href="/wiki/Asset_management" title="Asset management">asset management</a> services to encourage <a href="/wiki/Private_sector_development" title="Private sector development">private sector development</a> in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>. The IFC is a member of the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank_Group" title="World Bank Group">World Bank Group</a> and is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It was established in 1956 as the private sector arm of the World Bank Group to advance <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> by investing in strictly for-profit and commercial projects that purport to <a href="/wiki/Poverty_reduction" title="Poverty reduction">reduce poverty</a> and promote development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ottenhoff_2011_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ottenhoff_2011-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_History_2012_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_History_2012-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madura_2007_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Madura_2007-3">[3]</a></sup> The IFC's stated aim is to create opportunities for people to escape poverty and achieve better living standards by mobilizing financial resources for private enterprise, promoting accessible and competitive markets, supporting businesses and other private sector entities, and creating jobs and delivering necessary services to those who are poverty-stricken or otherwise vulnerable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Vision_2012_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Vision_2012-4">[4]</a></sup> Since 2009, the IFC has focused on a set of development goals that its projects are expected to target. Its goals are to increase <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a> opportunities, improve <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, increase access to financing for <a href="/wiki/Microfinance" title="Microfinance">microfinance</a> and business clients, advance <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>, help small businesses grow revenues, and invest in <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate</a> health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Dev_Goals_2012_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Dev_Goals_2012-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IFC is owned and governed by its member countries, but has its own executive leadership and staff that conduct its normal business operations. It is a <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a> whose <a href="/wiki/Shareholder" title="Shareholder">shareholders</a> are member governments that provide <a href="/wiki/Paid_in_capital" title="Paid in capital">paid-in capital</a> and which have the right to vote on its matters. Originally more financially integrated with the World Bank Group, the IFC was established separately and eventually became authorized to operate as a financially autonomous entity and make independent investment decisions. It offers an array of <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Equity_(finance)" title="Equity (finance)">equity</a> financing services and helps companies face their risk exposures, while refraining from participating in a <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a> capacity. The corporation also offers advice to companies on making decisions, evaluating their impact on the environment and society, and being responsible. It advises governments on building infrastructure and partnerships to further support private sector development.</p>
<p>The corporation is assessed by an independent evaluator each year. In 2011, its evaluation report recognized that its investments performed well and reduced poverty, but recommended that the corporation define poverty and expected outcomes more explicitly to better-understand its effectiveness and approach poverty reduction more strategically. The corporation's total investments in 2011 amounted to $18.66 billion. It committed $820 million to advisory services for 642 projects in 2011, and held $24.5 billion worth of liquid assets. The IFC is in good financial standing and received the highest ratings from two independent <a href="/wiki/Credit_rating_agency" title="Credit rating agency">credit rating agencies</a> in 2010 and 2011.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:International_Finance_Corporation_Building.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2434" data-file-width="3072" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/International_Finance_Corporation_Building.JPG/200px-International_Finance_Corporation_Building.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/International_Finance_Corporation_Building.JPG/300px-International_Finance_Corporation_Building.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/International_Finance_Corporation_Building.JPG/400px-International_Finance_Corporation_Building.JPG 2x" width="200" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:International_Finance_Corporation_Building.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> were designed by delegates at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Monetary_and_Financial_Conference" title="United Nations Monetary and Financial Conference">Bretton Woods conference</a> in 1944 and the World Bank, then consisting of only the <a href="/wiki/International_Bank_for_Reconstruction_and_Development" title="International Bank for Reconstruction and Development">International Bank for Reconstruction and Development</a>, became operational in 1946. Robert L. Garner joined the World Bank in 1947 as a senior executive and expressed his view that private business could play an important role in international development. In 1950, Garner and his colleagues proposed establishing a new institution for the purpose of making private investments in the developing countries served by the Bank. The U.S. government encouraged the idea of an international corporation working in tandem with the World Bank to invest in private enterprises without accepting guarantees from governments, without managing those enterprises, and by collaborating with third party investors. When describing the IFC in 1955, World Bank President Eugene R. Black said that the IFC would only invest in private firms, rather than make loans to governments, and it would not manage the projects in which it invests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacks_1955_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jacks_1955-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1956 the International Finance Corporation became operational under the leadership of Garner. It initially had 12 staff members and $100 million ($844.9 million in 2012 dollars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLS_2012_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLS_2012-7">[7]</a></sup>) in capital. The corporation made its inaugural investment in 1957 by making a $2 million ($16.4 million in 2012 dollars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLS_2012_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-BLS_2012-7">[7]</a></sup>) loan to a Brazil-based affiliate of <a href="/wiki/Siemens_%26_Halske" title="Siemens &amp; Halske">Siemens & Halske</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens AG</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_History_2012_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_History_2012-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2007, IFC bought 18% stake in the Indian Financial firm, <a href="/wiki/Angel_Broking" title="Angel Broking">Angel Broking</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In December 2015 IFC supported <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Greece" title="Economy of Greece">Greek</a> banks with 150 million euros by buying shares in four of them: <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Bank" title="Alpha Bank">Alpha Bank</a> (60 million), <a href="/wiki/Eurobank_Ergasias" title="Eurobank Ergasias">Eurobank</a> (50 million), <a href="/wiki/Piraeus_Bank" title="Piraeus Bank">Piraeus Bank</a> (20 million) and <a href="/wiki/National_Bank_of_Greece" title="National Bank of Greece">National Bank of Greece</a> (20 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The IFC is governed by its Board of Governors which meets annually and consists of one governor per member country (most often the country's finance minister or treasury secretary).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ottenhoff_2011_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ottenhoff_2011-1">[1]</a></sup> Each member typically appoints one governor and also one alternate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2010_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2010-10">[10]</a></sup> Although corporate authority rests with the Board of Governors, the governors delegate most of their corporate powers and their authority over daily matters such as lending and business operations to the Board of Directors. The IFC's Board of Directors consists of 25 executive directors who meet regularly and work at the IFC's headquarters, and is chaired by the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank_Group#Presidency" title="World Bank Group">President of the World Bank Group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2011_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2011-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Organizational_Chart_2012_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Organizational_Chart_2012-12">[12]</a></sup> The executive directors collectively represent all 184 member countries. When the IFC's Board of Directors votes on matters brought before it, each executive director's vote is weighted according to the total share capital of the member countries represented by that director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2011_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2011-11">[11]</a></sup> The IFC's Executive Vice President and CEO oversees its overall direction and daily operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ottenhoff_2011_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ottenhoff_2011-1">[1]</a></sup> As of October 2012, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jin-Yong_Cai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jin-Yong Cai (page does not exist)">Jin-Yong Cai</a> serves as the Executive Vice President and CEO of the IFC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Organizational_Chart_2012_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Organizational_Chart_2012-12">[12]</a></sup> President of the World Bank Group <a href="/wiki/Jim_Yong_Kim" title="Jim Yong Kim">Jim Yong Kim</a> appointed <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jin-Yong_Cai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jin-Yong Cai (page does not exist)">Jin-Yong Cai</a> to serve as the new Executive Vice President and CEO of the IFC. Cai is a Chinese citizen who formerly served as a managing director for <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> and has over 20 years of financial sector experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_2012_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters_2012-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Indian_Express_2012_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Indian_Express_2012-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the IFC coordinates its activities in many areas with the other World Bank Group institutions, it generally operates independently as it is a separate entity with legal and financial autonomy, established by its own Articles of Agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2011_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2011-11">[11]</a></sup> The corporation operates with a staff of over 3,400 employees, of which half are stationed in field offices across its member nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ottenhoff_2011_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ottenhoff_2011-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Services">Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Investment_services">Investment services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Investment services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The IFC's investment services consist of <a href="/wiki/Loan" title="Loan">loans</a>, equity, <a href="/wiki/Trade_finance" title="Trade finance">trade finance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syndicated_loan" title="Syndicated loan">syndicated loans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structured_finance" title="Structured finance">structured</a> and <a href="/wiki/Securitization" title="Securitization">securitized</a> finance, client <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">risk management</a> services, <a href="/wiki/Treasury_services" title="Treasury services">treasury services</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Market_liquidity" title="Market liquidity">liquidity</a> management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2010_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2010-10">[10]</a></sup> In its fiscal year 2010, the IFC invested $12.7 billion in 528 projects across 103 countries. Of that total investment commitment, approximately 39% ($4.9 billion) was invested into 255 projects across 58 member nations of the World Bank's <a href="/wiki/International_Development_Association" title="International Development Association">International Development Association</a> (IDA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2010_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2010-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IFC makes loans to businesses and private projects generally with maturities of seven to twelve years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2010_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2010-10">[10]</a></sup> It determines a suitable repayment schedule and grace period for each loan individually to meet borrowers' currency and cash flow requirements. The IFC may provide longer-term loans or extend grace periods if a project is deemed to warrant it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Loans_2012_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Loans_2012-15">[15]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leasing" title="Leasing">Leasing</a> companies and <a href="/wiki/Financial_intermediary" title="Financial intermediary">financial intermediaries</a> may also receive loans from the IFC. Though loans have traditionally been denominated in <a href="/wiki/Hard_currency" title="Hard currency">hard currencies</a>, the IFC has endeavored to structure loan products in local currencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Local_Currency_2012_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Local_Currency_2012-16">[16]</a></sup> Its disbursement portfolio included loans denominated in 25 local currencies in 2010, and 45 local currencies in 2011, funded largely through swap markets. Local financial markets development is one of IFC’s strategic focus areas. In line with its AAA rating, it has strict concentration, liquidity, asset-liability and other policies. The IFC committed to approximately $5.7 billion in new loans in 2010, and $5 billion in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2010_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2010-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2011_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2011-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the IFC's shareholders initially only allowed it to make loans, the IFC was authorized in 1961 to make equity investments, the first of which was made in 1962 by taking a stake in FEMSA, a former manufacturer of auto parts in Spain that is now part of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bosch_GmbH" title="Robert Bosch GmbH">Bosch Spain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_History_2012_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_History_2012-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bosch_Spain_2012_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bosch_Spain_2012-17">[17]</a></sup> The IFC invests in businesses' equity either directly or via <a href="/wiki/Private_equity_fund" title="Private equity fund">private equity funds</a>, generally from five up to twenty percent of a company's total equity. IFC’s private equity portfolio currently stands at roughly $3.0 billion committed to about 180 funds. The portfolio is widely distributed across all regions including Africa, East Asia, South Asia, Eastern Europe, Latin America and the Middle East, and recently has invested in <a href="/wiki/Small_Enterprise_Assistance_Funds" title="Small Enterprise Assistance Funds">Small Enterprise Assistance Funds</a>' (SEAF) <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> Growth Fund,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Aureos Capital's Kula Fund II (<a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">Pacific Islands</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Leopard_Capital" title="Leopard Capital">Leopard Capital</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> Fund.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Other equity investments made by the IFC include <a href="/wiki/Preferred_stock" title="Preferred stock">preferred equity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convertible_bond" title="Convertible bond">convertible loans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Participation_loan" title="Participation loan">participation loans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2010_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2010-10">[10]</a></sup> The IFC prefers to invest for the long-term, usually for a period of eight to fifteen years, before exiting through the sale of shares on a domestic <a href="/wiki/Stock_exchange" title="Stock exchange">stock exchange</a>, usually as part of an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>. When the IFC invests in a company, it does not assume an active role in management of the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Equity_2012_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Equity_2012-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through its Global Trade Finance Program, the IFC guarantees trade payment obligations of more than 200 approved banks in over 80 countries to mitigate risk for international transactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2011_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2011-11">[11]</a></sup> The Global Trade Finance Program provides guarantees to cover payment risks for emerging market banks regarding promissory notes, bills of exchange, letters of credit, bid and performance bonds, supplier credit for capital goods imports, and advance payments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Global_Trade_Finance_2012_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Global_Trade_Finance_2012-22">[22]</a></sup> The IFC issued $3.46 billion in more than 2,800 guarantees in 2010, of which over 51% targeted IDA member nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2010_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2010-10">[10]</a></sup> In its fiscal year 2011, the IFC issued $4.6 billion in more than 3,100 guarantees. In 2009, the IFC launched a separate program for crisis response, known as its Global Trade Liquidity Program, which provides liquidity for international trade among developing countries. Since its establishment in 2009, the Global Trade Liquidity Program assisted with over $15 billion in trade in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2011_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2011-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IFC operates a Syndicated Loan Program in an effort to mobilize capital for development goals. The program was created in 1957 and as of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> has channeled approximately $38 billion from over 550 financial institutions toward development projects in over 100 different <a href="/wiki/Emerging_markets" title="Emerging markets">emerging markets</a>. The IFC syndicated a total of $4.7 billion in loans in 2011, twice that of its $2 billion worth of syndications in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2010_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2010-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2011_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2011-11">[11]</a></sup> Due to banks retrenching from lending across borders in emerging markets, in 2009 the IFC started to syndicate parallel loans to the international financial institutions and other participants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Syndications_2012_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Syndications_2012-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>To service clients without ready access to low-cost financing, the IFC relies on structured or securitized financial products such as partial credit guarantees, portfolio risk transfers, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_economic_jurisprudence" title="Islamic economic jurisprudence">Islamic finance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2011_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2011-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Structured_Finance_2012_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Structured_Finance_2012-24">[24]</a></sup> The IFC committed $797 million in the form of structured and securitized financing in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2010_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2010-10">[10]</a></sup> For companies that face difficulty in obtaining financing due to a perception of high <a href="/wiki/Credit_risk" title="Credit risk">credit risk</a>, the IFC securitizes assets with predictable cash flows, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mortgage" title="Mortgage">mortgages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Credit_card" title="Credit card">credit cards</a>, loans, <a href="/wiki/Corporate_bond" title="Corporate bond">corporate debt</a> instruments, and revenue streams, in an effort to enhance those companies' credit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Securitizations_2012_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Securitizations_2012-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Financial <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">derivative</a> products are made available to the IFC's clients strictly for <a href="/wiki/Hedge_(finance)" title="Hedge (finance)">hedging</a> <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate_risk" title="Interest rate risk">interest rate risk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foreign_exchange_risk" title="Foreign exchange risk">exchange rate risk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Commodity_risk" title="Commodity risk">commodity risk exposure</a>. It serves as an intermediary between emerging market businesses and international derivatives <a href="/wiki/Market_maker" title="Market maker">market makers</a> to increase access to risk management instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2011_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2011-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Risk_Management_2012_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Risk_Management_2012-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IFC fulfills a treasury role by borrowing international capital to fund lending activities. It is usually one of the first institutions to issue bonds or to do swaps in emerging markets denominated in those markets' local currencies. The IFC's new international borrowings amounted to $8.8 billion in 2010 and $9.8 billion in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2010_10-9"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2010-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2011_11-9"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2011-11">[11]</a></sup> The IFC Treasury actively engages in liquidity management in an effort to maximize returns and assure that funding for its investments is readily available while managing risks to the IFC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Liquidity_Management_2012_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Liquidity_Management_2012-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advisory_services">Advisory services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Advisory services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to its investment activities the IFC provides a range of advisory services to support corporate decisionmaking regarding business, environment, social impact, and sustainability. The IFC's corporate advice targets governance, managerial capacity, scalability, and corporate responsibility. It prioritizes the encouragement of reforms that improve the trade friendliness and ease of doing business in an effort to advise countries on fostering a suitable investment climate. It also offers advice to governments on infrastructure development and public-private partnerships. The IFC attempts to guide businesses toward more sustainable practices particularly with regards to having good governance, supporting women in business, and proactively combating climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Annual_Report_2011_11-10"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Annual_Report_2011-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asset_Management_Company">Asset Management Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Asset Management Company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The IFC established IFC Asset Management Company LLC (IFC AMC) in 2009 as a wholly owned <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">subsidiary</a> to manage all capital funds to be invested in emerging markets. The AMC manages capital mobilized by the IFC as well as by third parties such as sovereign or pension funds, and other development financing organizations. Despite being owned by the IFC, the AMC has investment decision autonomy and is charged with a <a href="/wiki/Fiduciary" title="Fiduciary">fiduciary responsibility</a> to the four individual funds under its management. It also aims to mobilize additional capital for IFC investments as it can make certain types of investments which the IFC cannot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_AMC_2012_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_AMC_2012-28">[28]</a></sup> As of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the AMC managed the IFC Capitalization Fund (Equity) Fund, L.P., the IFC Capitalization (Subordinated Debt) Fund, L.P., the IFC African, Latin American, and Caribbean Fund, L.P., and the Africa Capitalization Fund, Ltd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Financials_.26_Projects_2011_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Financials_.26_Projects_2011-29">[29]</a></sup> The IFC Capitalization (Equity) Fund holds $1.3 billion in equity, while the IFC Capitalization (Subordinated Debt) Fund is valued at $1.7 billion. The IFC African, Latin American, and Caribbean Fund (referred to as the IFC ALAC Fund) was created in 2010 and is worth $1 billion. As of March 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the ALAC Fund has invested a total of $349.1 million into twelve businesses. The Africa Capitalization Fund was set up in 2011 to invest in commercial banks in both Northern and Sub-Saharan Africa and its commitments totaled $181.8 million in March 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_AMC_2012_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_AMC_2012-28">[28]</a></sup> As of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gavin_E.R._Wilson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gavin E.R. Wilson (page does not exist)">Gavin E.R. Wilson</a> serves as CEO of the AMC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Organizational_Chart_2012_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Organizational_Chart_2012-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_performance">Financial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Financial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The IFC prepares <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_financial_statement" title="Consolidated financial statement">consolidated financial statements</a> in accordance with United States <a href="/wiki/Generally_accepted_accounting_principles" title="Generally accepted accounting principles">GAAP</a> which are audited by <a href="/wiki/KPMG" title="KPMG">KPMG</a>. It reported income before grants to IDA members of $2.18 billion in fiscal year 2011, up from $1.95 billion in fiscal 2010 and $299 million in fiscal 2009. The increase in income before grants is ascribed to higher earnings from the IFC's investments and also from higher service fees. The IFC reported a partial offset from lower liquid asset trading income, higher administrative costs, and higher advisory service expenses. The IFC made $600 million in grants to IDA countries in fiscal 2011, up from $200 million in fiscal 2010 and $450 million in fiscal 2009. The IFC reported a <a href="/wiki/Net_income" title="Net income">net income</a> of $1.58 billion in fiscal year 2011. In previous years, the IFC had reported a net loss of $151 million in fiscal 2009 and $1.75 billion in fiscal 2010. The IFC's total capital amounted to $20.3 billion in 2011, of which $2.4 billion was paid-in capital from member countries, $16.4 billion was retained earnings, and $1.5 billion was <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_other_comprehensive_income" title="Accumulated other comprehensive income">accumulated other comprehensive income</a>. The IFC held $68.49 billion in total assets in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Financials_.26_Projects_2011_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Financials_.26_Projects_2011-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IFC's <a href="/wiki/Return_on_assets" title="Return on assets">return on average assets</a> (GAAP basis) decreased from 3.1% in 2010 to 2.4% in 2011. Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Return_on_average_capital_employed" title="Return on average capital employed">return on average capital</a> (GAAP basis) decreased from 10.1% in 2010 to 8.2% in 2011. The IFC's cash and liquid investments accounted for 83% of its estimated net <a href="/wiki/Cash_flow" title="Cash flow">cash</a> requirements for fiscal years 2012 through 2014. Its external funding liquidity level grew from 190% in 2010 to 266% in 2011. It has a 2.6:1 <a href="/wiki/Debt-to-equity_ratio" title="Debt-to-equity ratio">debt-to-equity ratio</a> and holds 6.6% in reserves against losses on loans to its disbursement portfolio. The IFC's deployable strategic capital decreased from 14% in 2010 to 10% in 2011 as a share of its total resources available, which grew from $16.8 billion in 2010 to $17.9 billion in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Financials_.26_Projects_2011_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Financials_.26_Projects_2011-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, the IFC reported total funding commitments (consisting of loans, equity, guarantees, and client risk management) of $12.18 billion, slightly lower than its $12.66 billion in commitments in 2010. Its core mobilization, which consists of participation and parallel loans, structured finance, its Asset Management Company funds, and other initiatives, grew from $5.38 billion in 2010 to $6.47 billion in 2011. The IFC's total investment program was reported at a value of $18.66 billion for fiscal year 2011. Its advisory services portfolio included 642 projects valued at $820 million in 2011, compared to 736 projects at $859 million in 2010. The IFC held $24.5 billion in liquid assets in 2011, up from $21 billion in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFC_Financials_.26_Projects_2011_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-IFC_Financials_.26_Projects_2011-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IFC received <a href="/wiki/Credit_rating" title="Credit rating">credit ratings</a> of AAA from <a href="/wiki/Standard_%26_Poor%27s" title="Standard &amp; Poor's">Standard & Poor's</a> in December 2012 and Aaa from <a href="/wiki/Moody%27s_Investors_Service" title="Moody's Investors Service">Moody's Investors Service</a> in November 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swann_.26_Chambers_2012_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swann_.26_Chambers_2012-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hess_et_al._2012_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hess_et_al._2012-31">[31]</a></sup> S&P rated the IFC as having a strong financial standing with adequate capital and liquidity, cautious management policies, a high level of geographic diversification, and anticipated treatment as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preferred_creditor" title="Preferred creditor">preferred creditor</a> given its membership in the World Bank Group. It noted that the IFC faces a weakness relative to other multilateral institutions of having higher risks due to its mandated emphasis on private sector investing and its income heavily affected by equity markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swann_.26_Chambers_2010_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swann_.26_Chambers_2010-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_Buildings_in_Developing_Countries">Green Buildings in Developing Countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Finance_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Green Buildings in Developing Countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="external text" href="http://www.ifc.org" rel="nofollow">IFC</a> has created a mass market certification system for fast growing Emerging Markets called <a class="external text" href="http://www.ifc.org/edge" rel="nofollow">EDGE ("Excellence in Design for Greater Efficiencies")</a>. IFC and the <a class="external text" href="http://www.worldgbc.org/activities/edge-system/" rel="nofollow">World Green Building Council</a> have partnered to accelerate green building growth in developing counties. The target is to scale up green buildings over a seven-year period until 20% of the property market is saturated]. Certification occurs when the <a class="external text" href="https://www.edgebuildings.com" rel="nofollow">EDGE</a> standard is met, which requires 20% less energy, water, and materials than conventional homes.</p>
